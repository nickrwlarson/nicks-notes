Meditations by Marcus Aurelius

January 10, 2025 · 5 min read

Marcus Aurelius wrote Meditations for himself. There's no audience being addressed, no argument being made, no reputation being managed. It's a man at the height of worldly power quietly reminding himself not to be an idiot. That's what makes it unlike anything else in philosophy.

The Central Idea

Aurelius returns again and again to a single insight: you cannot control what happens to you, only how you respond. This sounds like a bumper sticker. In his hands, it becomes a complete operating system for life.

You have power over your mind, not outside events. Realize this, and you will find strength.

The Stoics believed that our emotions aren't caused by events — they're caused by our judgments about events. Change the judgment, change the emotion. This isn't repression; it's precision.

What Stuck With Me

The book's power comes from its honesty. Aurelius is clearly struggling. He writes the same lessons to himself dozens of times across twelve books, which suggests he kept forgetting them. There's something deeply reassuring about that — the most powerful man in the world, working on the same things we all work on.

Small rituals matter

Several passages deal with how to start the day — expecting difficulty, preparing mentally, not being surprised when people are frustrating. Two thousand years before productivity culture, Aurelius had a morning routine. It was mostly just reminding himself to be patient.

Verdict

One of the five books I'd take to a desert island. It doesn't matter what you do for a living or where you are in life — this one has something for you. Read Gregory Hays' translation. It's the most readable by a significant margin and makes the whole thing feel like it was written last week.
